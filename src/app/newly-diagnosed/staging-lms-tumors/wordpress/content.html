Understanding LMS Staging

The process of determining how far cancer has spread

The process of finding out how far leiomyosarcoma has spread is called staging. In sarcoma staging, doctors evaluate the appearance of the tumor under the microscope and judge how fast the cancer seems to be growing. The stage of a sarcoma is the most significant factor in determining each patient's prognosis and selecting treatment options.

Staging Process

  • Biopsies examination
  • Imaging tests of main tumor (CT/MRI)
  • Evaluation of potential spread
  • Microscopic tissue analysis

Genetic Testing Note

Molecular/genomic testing for sarcoma-related genes may not be for staging purposes but can help identify potential targeted therapy options. Ask your doctor if genetic testing of your tumor is recommended.

TNM System & Grading

Understanding how LMS is classified and staged

TNM Staging System

T

Size of the tumor

N

Spread to lymph nodes (small bean-shaped collections of immune system cells found throughout the body that help fight infections and cancers)

M

Metastasis (spread to distant organs)

Grade (G) Factors

The grade of a tumor is determined by examining the cancer cells under a microscope and scoring three factors:

Differentiation

Cancer cells are given a score of 1 to 3, with 1 being assigned when they look similar to normal cells and 3 being used when the cancer cells look very abnormal

Mitotic count

How many cancer cells are seen dividing under the microscope; given a score from 1 to 3 (a lower score means fewer cells were seen dividing)

Tumor necrosis

How much of the tumor is made up of dying tissue; given a score from 0 to 2 (a lower score means there was less dying tissue present)

The scores for each factor are added to determine the grade. A total score of 2-3 is Grade 1 (low grade), 4-5 is Grade 2 (intermediate grade), and 6-8 is Grade 3 (high grade).

Stage 1 (1A/1B grade) Low-Grade tumor Surgery. Recommendations for more treatment is based on size of margin is 1 cm or less and if the fascia was cut. Clean margins around the tumor site (always the goal) Will begin when treatment is completed. Rehabilitation if needed Imaging – CT Scan, CT/PET scan Note: CT scan for lung surveillance is best Genetic test of the tumor Regular imaging of primary tumor site Follow up appointments with blood work done

Surgery to remove metastases with or without chemotherapy before or after surgery; with or without radiation therapy – such as Ablation, SBRT, embolization, other modalities; observation Rehabilitation if needed Imaging tests for surveillance same cycles as indicated for Stages 2 and 3. Recurrence

Understanding and treating LMS recurrence

Even after successful treatment, LMS can sometimes recur (come back). The approach to treating recurrent LMS depends on where the cancer returns and how widespread it is.

Local Recurrence

Cancer returns at or near the original site

Treatment Approach

Testing; Treatment based on stage (extent of disease), More treatment as listed above

Isolated Recurrence

Regional disease or lymph nodes

Treatment Approach

Regional node dissection with or without radiation therapy, with or without chemotherapy SBRT Isolated limb infusion/per-fusion plus surgery

Widespread Recurrence

Cancer has spread to multiple distant sites

Treatment Approach

Palliative chemotherapy, Palliative radiation therapy SBRT, ablation, embolization. Observation if no symptoms
